MARINE

Essentially, Malaysia is a maritime nation. More than 19 degrees longitude, with more than 4,600 kilometres of coastline and four big large bodies of water surrounding it (Straits of Malacca, South China Sea, Sulu Sea and Sulawesi Sea).

OVERVIEW

Malaysia has more sea territories (including the claimed EEZ) under its jurisdiction than land territory. In Southeast Asia, Malaysians extract more protein from fish than any other nation (World Resources Institute 2006). Sections of the seas of Malaysia fall within two ecoregions of the Global 200 (Andaman Sea Ecoregion, Sulu-Sulawesi Sea Ecoregion) and the ecological Reef Triangle is an integral part of the country. Marine restoration and sustainable management of marine resources are relevant in so many areas of the sea.






SULU-SULAWESI MARINE ECOREGION (SSME) PROGRAMME

The SSME programme focuses on two priority protected areas, Kudat-Banggi and Semporna, as well as on the trade in live reef fish. Habitat goals among these PCAs include the two highest concentrations of coral reefs and seagrasses in Malaysia and substantial mangrove forests. These projects are aimed at improving natural resource conservation for all stakeholders, including local governments, the private sector and government agencies.






TUN MUSTAPHA PARK (TMP)

With more than 50 islands and islets, the planned Tun Mustapha Park (TMP) measures nearly 1 million hectares and will be the largest marine protected area and the first multiple-use park in Malaysia. It will be managed in cooperation with local stakeholders to conserve and maintain the valuable resources found within the aquatic and coastal habitats.






SEA TURTLES

These friendly marine reptiles swim large distances and come to shore only to nest on the beaches of Malaysia. Within local communities, they are recognised for their longevity. Unfortunately, the number of sea turtles has declined in most countries and some species are on the verge of extinction. At Rantau Abang, Terengganu, some 10,000 leatherback turtle nests were documented in the 1950s. No nesting of leatherbacks has been documented since 2011. We can not afford to let the same fate be met by our hawksbill, olive ridley and green turtles.




Malaysian Wildlife works with local communities, local and state government agencies and the private sector to improve management of important and threatened ecosystems and species.
